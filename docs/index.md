# Debugging Guide 2026

---

## ◎ What Is This Guide?

This is a **practical, progressive guide** to debugging software. It teaches debugging as a **transferable skill** — one that works across programming languages, frameworks, and environments.

The guide is organised by **debugging maturity**, not by academic year or programming language. You do not need to read it from start to finish. Navigate to the section that matches where you are right now.

---

## ◎ Symbol Key

These symbols appear throughout the guide to indicate which level of programmer a piece of content is primarily aimed at.

| Symbol | Level | Who It Is For |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| `◉` | **Foundational** | New programmers learning to read errors and follow a basic workflow |
| `◈` | **Investigative** | Programmers who can write code but struggle with complex or indirect bugs |
| `◆` | **Strategic** | Advanced students, final-year projects, and junior developers in the workplace |
| `◎` | **Cross-Cutting** | Concepts and techniques that apply to all levels |
| `▣` | **Exercise** | Practical activities to apply and test your understanding |
| `⌨` | **Platform Note** | Environment-specific guidance (IDE, terminal, or platform) |

Content marked `◎` is worth reading regardless of your level. Content marked `▣` is hands-on — do not skip it.

## ◎ Core Principle

> **The computer is not wrong.**
> It is doing exactly what it was told to do.
> An error means something about your instructions was unclear, incomplete, or incorrect.

**Errors are feedback, not failure.**

---

## ◎ A Note on AI Tools

AI tools — including those integrated directly into your IDE — can be useful debugging assistants. But they are not a replacement for understanding your own code.

A fix you cannot explain is not a fix. It is temporary luck.

This guide will show you how to use AI tools effectively as part of a broader debugging process — not as a shortcut around one.

---

**Debugging is not about fixing mistakes. It is about understanding systems.**
